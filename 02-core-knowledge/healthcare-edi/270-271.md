# 270/271 Eligibility Inquiry & Response

**30-Second Explanation**: 270 asks "Does this patient have active insurance today?" The 271 answers "Yes, and their copay is $20" OR "No, this policy is inactive / Member Not Found."

**2-Minute Explanation**: This is the most critical real-time transaction in healthcare. Before a doctor provides care, the receptionist submits a 270 containing the patient's name, DOB, and Member ID. The Payer instantly returns a 271. If this fails, the hospital might perform an expensive procedure on an uninsured patient and never get paid. Because it happens while the patient is standing at the front desk, the SLA for turnaround time is usually under 2-3 seconds.

**Purpose**: To verify active coverage and determine patient financial responsibility (copays/deductibles) before rendering services.

**Who sends it**: Providers (270). Payers (271).

**Who receives it**: Payers (270). Providers (271).

**Input**: Member ID, DOB, Name, Date of Service.

**Output**: Active/Inactive status, Copay, Deductible, Co-insurance amounts.

**Common Errors**: 
- Name misspelling or DOB mismatch causing an "AAA" error (Member Not Found).
- API timeouts on the payer side causing the provider to assume the patient is uninsured.

**Real Project Example**: The architecture I supported at Humana for the 276/277 (real-time synchronous HTTP, 2.5s SLA, heavy thread tuning) is exactly the same architectural pattern used for massive 270/271 eligibility pipelines.

**Interview Questions**:
- *Why is high availability and low latency absolutely critical for 270/271?* (Answer: Because it is a point-of-care transaction. If the system is down, patients standing at the front desk cannot be admitted or treated).
