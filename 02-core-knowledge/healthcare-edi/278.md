# 278 Prior Authorization

**30-Second Explanation**: Asking the insurance company for permission before performing an expensive procedure.

**2-Minute Explanation**: For high-cost procedures (MRIs, surgeries, specialty drugs), a provider cannot just do the work and bill the payer later. They must submit a 278 Request containing the diagnosis and procedure codes. The payer reviews the request (often against strict medical policies) and sends a 278 Response granting or denying authorization. If the provider skips this step, the subsequent 837 claim will be automatically denied.

**Purpose**: Cost control for payers and guarantee of payment for providers.

**Who sends it**: Providers.

**Who receives it**: Payers (often Utilization Management departments).

**Input**: Patient demographics, Diagnosis Codes (ICD-10), Procedure Codes (CPT).

**Output**: An Authorization Number (if approved) or a denial reason.

**Common Errors**: 
- Provider submitting the 837 claim but forgetting to include the Authorization Number generated by the 278.
- Missing clinical attachments (which usually require a 275 transaction to send the actual PDF medical records).

**Real Project Example**: Integrating 278s often requires complex DataFlows in Edifecs because the payload must frequently be routed to a human workflow system (like a nurse review queue) rather than being fully auto-adjudicated.

**Interview Questions**:
- *What is the relationship between a 278 and an 837?* (Answer: If a procedure requires prior auth, the 278 must be approved first. The resulting Authorization Number must then be printed on the 837 claim, or the claim will be denied).
