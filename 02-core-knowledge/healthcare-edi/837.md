# 837 Health Care Claim

**30-Second Explanation**: The actual medical bill. It is the X12 file sent by a hospital or doctor to an insurance company demanding payment for services rendered.

**2-Minute Explanation**: The 837 is the most complex and critical transaction in healthcare. It contains everything: who the patient is, who the doctor is, what the diagnosis was (ICD-10), what procedure was performed (CPT), and how much it costs. It comes in three main flavors: 837I (Institutional/Hospitals), 837P (Professional/Doctors), and 837D (Dental). 

**Purpose**: To submit a formal request for payment from a provider to a payer.

**Who sends it**: Providers (usually batched via Clearinghouses).

**Who receives it**: Payers.

**Input**: Medical coding data from an EHR (Epic/Cerner).

**Output**: Triggers adjudication on the Payer side, leading to an 835 (payment).

**Common Errors**: 
- "Member Not Found" (Name or DOB mismatch).
- Invalid Diagnosis codes.
- Missing Rendering Provider NPI.

**Real Project Example**: For the State of Colorado, I was responsible for mapping complex 837I (Institutional) claims. I used Edifecs MapBuilder to translate the deeply nested X12 hierarchical loops (Billing Provider -> Subscriber -> Patient -> Claim -> Service Line) into a flat XML structure required by the state's Medicaid adjudication system.

**Interview Questions**:
- *What are the three main types of 837s?* (Answer: Institutional, Professional, Dental).
- *Describe the hierarchical loop structure of an 837.* (Answer: 2000A Billing Provider -> 2000B Subscriber -> 2000C Patient -> 2300 Claim -> 2400 Service Line).
